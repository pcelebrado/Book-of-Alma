Most traders watch price and miss the lever beneath it.

They see the candle close higher and assume strength. They see volatility compress and assume calm. They see the same chart pattern that worked last month and assume it will work again. But beneath the surface, something more fundamental is shifting: the relationship between volatility and delta exposure, governed by second-order Greeks that most market participants barely understand.

This is the hidden machinery of markets. Volatility can move delta pressure without asking spot for permission. A market that looks stable can become unstable in seconds when the wrong combination of vanna and speed aligns. The trader who sees only price is watching the puppet. The trader who understands second-order Greeks is watching the strings.

## Definitions Used in This Chapter

Before we dive into the mechanics, let us establish clear definitions:

- **Vanna:** The sensitivity of delta to changes in implied volatility (∂Δ/∂σ). Vanna is what makes IV direction matter for spot hedging flows. When volatility shifts, vanna determines how dealer delta exposure changes—and therefore, what hedging trades dealers must execute.

- **Speed:** The rate of change of gamma with respect to spot (∂Γ/∂S). Speed is what makes a "stable" gamma map become unstable quickly. It governs whether gamma concentrates or dilutes as price moves.

- **Volatility surface:** Implied volatility as a function of strike and maturity. Vanna and speed behave differently across this surface, creating complex interaction effects that simplistic models miss.

- **Dealer positioning versus option Greeks:** The market's behavior is driven by aggregated dealer inventory and hedging constraints across thousands of contracts and multiple tenors—not by a single option's Greek printed on a screen.

## 5.0 Why Second-Order Greeks Matter

Gamma explains why dealers can dampen or amplify spot moves. Vanna and speed explain why the *same* spot move behaves differently on two days that look identical on a chart.

Consider this scenario: Monday and Tuesday both open at the same level, print similar candles, and face the same macro headlines. But on Monday, implied volatility drifts lower, while on Tuesday, it snaps higher. The dealer book shifts its hedge pressure without spot doing anything dramatic on either day. Yet the market behavior is completely different—Monday's moves get absorbed, while Tuesday's moves accelerate.

This is the lever you are trying to see.

Alma is openly hostile toward what he calls "Greek theater"—the tendency among retail traders and social media furus to treat dealer positioning as a mystical master key that explains every tick. His point is practical and grounded: a useful positioning model is a tool to derive *conditional* pivots and distributions, not a license to explain every price movement after the fact.^[Source: Alma, "Liquidity structure | Let's put speed profile into context" (Substack), bible/corpus/substack/unknown-date-liquidity-structure-let-s-put-speed-profile-into-context.md]

The academic literature supports this measured approach. Research on option market making with hedging-induced market impact (Aubert, Chevalier & Ly Vath, 2025) demonstrates that dealer hedging activity generates feedback loops between option trading and underlying price dynamics.^[arXiv:2511.02518v1] These effects are real and measurable, but they are contingent on inventory state, volatility regime, and market microstructure—not deterministic prophecies.

**Trading Implication:** If your setup depends on "the level holding," you need to know whether IV is making the dealer book more stabilizing or more destabilizing at that level. Size small, define your invalidation level before entry, and assume the regime can flip if IV moves against your position. Never risk more than you can afford to lose in a single trade.

## 5.1 Understanding Speed

Speed tells you how quickly gamma itself is changing as spot moves. That makes it a regime accelerator: a market that appears stable can become unstable fast when speed is unfavorable.

Imagine you are driving on a highway. Your speedometer shows a constant sixty miles per hour, so you assume the journey will be smooth. But what the speedometer does not show is the curvature of the road ahead. A gentle curve at sixty is manageable. A hairpin turn at sixty is catastrophic. Speed in options trading is that curvature—it tells you whether the road ahead is straight or about to bend sharply.

Alma's caution on supportive versus suppressive flow starts here. A static Greek read can mislead if speed curvature is steep and the chain is uneven across tenors. In those setups, identical spot moves do not produce identical hedge demand. The second derivative is where many false confidence trades are born.

The distinction between long and short speed profiles matters for how dealers respond to spot movement. As Alma illustrates in his schematic example of skew-contract modeling, a negative speed profile—where market makers are net long OTM puts and net short OTM calls—generates different mechanical flows than the reverse configuration.^[Source: Alma, "OpEx Flows, supportive or suppressive vanna/charm?" (Substack), bible/corpus/substack/unknown-date-opex-flows-supportive-or-suppressive-vanna-charm-intraday-po.md]

But Alma repeatedly warns that this schematic modeling oversimplifies reality. Dealers hold thousands, if not millions, of contracts across multiple tenors, and the weights of vanna and charm vary across that exposure. The schematic is a teaching tool, not a trading system.

Speed governs the stability of gamma assumptions through spot movement. When speed is adverse, the relationship between spot and hedge demand becomes nonlinear. A move that triggered modest rebalancing yesterday may force aggressive adjustment today as convexity curvature steepens. This is why static level maps fail in volatile regimes: they assume constant sensitivity when speed is actively reshaping it.

**Key Insight:** Speed governs the stability of gamma assumptions through spot movement.

**Trading Implication:** When speed is adverse, reduce reliance on static level maps and increase emphasis on real-time flow confirmation. Size positions so that a speed-driven gamma acceleration cannot damage your account, and always know the price level at which your thesis is wrong.

### Speed and the Gamma Acceleration Trap

The danger of adverse speed manifests most painfully during what appears to be a normal market move. Consider a scenario where a trader has sized a position based on yesterday's gamma map, assuming that a 1% move in spot will require a certain amount of dealer hedging. If speed is favorable, the actual hedge demand may be less than expected as gamma dilutes. But if speed is adverse, the same 1% move can trigger cascading hedge demands as gamma concentrates.

This is the gamma acceleration trap: the second derivative works against you silently until it doesn't. The move that "should" have found support based on static levels blows through them because speed has concentrated gamma at lower strikes, forcing dealers to sell more futures as spot falls, which pushes spot lower, which triggers more gamma concentration. The feedback loop is mechanical, not emotional.

Alma's warning about speed profile context is specifically about this trap. When he cautions that "the net exposure on a short or long speed profile is not a straight indicator," he is warning against assuming that knowing the sign of speed is enough. The magnitude matters, the distribution across tenors matters, and the interaction with vanna and charm matters.

**Practical Speed Assessment:**

To assess speed conditions in real-time, observe three things:

First, compare gamma notional by strike. Are the largest gamma concentrations near current spot or far away? If they are nearby, speed effects will activate quickly. If they are distant, you have more room before speed curvature steepens.

Second, check gamma term structure. Is front-month gamma disproportionately large relative to back-months? Front-heavy gamma means speed effects will dominate near-term price action, while back-loaded gamma means the market can absorb larger moves before mechanical pressure builds.

Third, monitor how delta adjustments change as spot moves. Are dealers adjusting more aggressively as the move continues (speed adverse) or less aggressively (speed favorable)? This requires watching the tape with an understanding of positioning, but the pattern becomes visible with practice.

If you observe speed-adverse conditions developing, treat it as a volatility amplifier. Moves will extend further than standard gamma models suggest, and reversals will be sharper when they finally occur.

## 5.2 The Danger of Schematic Oversimplification

A recurring trap in options analysis is the tendency to reduce complex dealer positioning to cartoon diagrams. The schematic model—using only two skew contracts to represent entire positioning surfaces—creates narratives that feel clean and are often wrong.

Alma addresses this directly when discussing how traders model OpEx flows: "If something is supportive or suppressive is very much context dependent, and assuming flows by using only the OTM 'skew' contracts for schematic examples is not necessarily accurate."^[Source: Alma, "OpEx Flows, supportive or suppressive vanna/charm?" (Substack), bible/corpus/substack/unknown-date-opex-flows-supportive-or-suppressive-vanna-charm-intraday-po.md]

The problem runs deeper than mere approximation. When a trader assumes a "negative speed profile" based solely on net long OTM puts and net short OTM calls, they derive predictions about vanna and charm flows that may not materialize. The schematic suggests net positive charm and net negative vanna, but the actual dealer book contains thousands of contracts with varying weights across strikes and tenors. The centroid of the setup—that critical point where one scenario activates over another—depends on how vanna, gamma, and charm interfere with time and volatility across the entire surface.

This is why Alma emphasizes that supportive or suppressive flows come from *passive* flows, but these passive flows can be overwritten or interfered with by shadow Greek-generated flows. The net exposure on a short or long speed profile is not a straight indicator for judging vanna and charm flows because their weights differ across the chain and tenors, generating different impacts.

The academic research on market making confirms this complexity. Studies of algorithmic market making in dealer markets (Barzykin, Bergault & Guéant, 2021) show that dealers operate within inventory ranges where they internalize flow by adjusting quotes, but externalize risk through hedging when inventory exceeds tolerance thresholds.^[arXiv:2106.06974v6] This creates nonlinear responses that no two-contract schematic can capture.

**Key Insight:** Schematic models are teaching tools, not trading systems. The real book is a weighted surface, not a cartoon.

**Trading Implication:** When you catch yourself thinking in terms of simple "MM long puts / short calls" diagrams, pause and ask: what am I missing about the full surface? Reduce position size until you have flow confirmation from actual market behavior, and define the IV movement that would invalidate your schematic assumption.

## 5.3 Sign and Scope: A Technical Box

Before vanna and speed can inform trading decisions, you must establish sign conventions and conditioning parameters. Without these guardrails, flow mapping becomes astrology with Greek letters.

### Sign Convention for Vanna

Under the standard convention used throughout this chapter:

- **Positive vanna** (∂Δ/∂σ > 0): Delta increases as implied volatility rises.
- **Negative vanna** (∂Δ/∂σ < 0): Delta decreases as implied volatility rises.

**Important:** The sign of "dealer net vanna" is an output of a positioning aggregation tool (surface-weighted across all strikes and tenors) and cannot be safely inferred from a single-contract cartoon.

### Flow Mapping Depends on Two Variables

The directional hedge flow generated by vanna depends on:

1. **Dealer net vanna sign** in the relevant zone and tenor (positive or negative)
2. **Implied volatility direction** (rising or falling)

### The 2×2 Scenario Table

The following table maps how dealer delta exposure changes with IV movements and the resulting hedge trades required to re-hedge, consistent with Alma's September 18 OpEx analysis:

| Dealer Net Vanna | IV Rising | IV Falling |
|-----------------|-----------|------------|
| **Positive** | Dealers get longer delta → Sell futures to re-hedge | Dealers get shorter delta → Buy futures to re-hedge |
| **Negative** | Dealers get shorter delta → Buy futures to re-hedge | Dealers get longer delta → Sell futures to re-hedge |

**Anchor to Alma's Language:** As Alma explains in his September 18 OpEx flows post, "If vol is sold, negative vanna would make the dealer longer delta, making him sell futures, and vice versa."^[Source: Alma, "OpEx Flows, supportive or suppressive vanna/charm?" (Substack), bible/corpus/substack/unknown-date-opex-flows-supportive-or-suppressive-vanna-charm-intraday-po.md] This means when IV falls (vol is sold) and dealers hold negative net vanna, they become longer delta and must sell futures to re-hedge.

**Critical Conditioning:** This table assumes the dealer is *net* positioned as shown and that the relevant exposure concentrates in the zone where spot is trading. If the dealer holds mixed exposure across strikes, or if the dominant vanna sits far from current spot, the mechanical flow may not materialize.

### Sign Convention for Speed

Speed (∂Γ/∂S) determines how gamma concentration changes as spot moves:

- **Positive speed**: Gamma increases as spot rises (and decreases as spot falls). This concentrates hedging pressure into rallies and dilutes it in selloffs.
- **Negative speed**: Gamma increases as spot falls (and decreases as spot rises). This concentrates hedging pressure into selloffs and dilutes it in rallies.

**Practical Application:** When dealers hold net long OTM puts and net short OTM calls (negative speed profile), a selloff brings more gamma into play, potentially accelerating hedge selling. A rally reduces gamma concentration, potentially removing a brake on upside momentum. But remember: this is Alma's *schematic example* for teaching purposes. The actual speed profile of a multi-tenor book requires surface analysis, not skew-contract simplification.

### Conditioning Checklist

Before executing any trade based on vanna or speed assumptions, verify:

1. **Zone alignment:** Is the dominant exposure concentrated where spot is trading, or is it distributed across distant strikes?
2. **Tenor consistency:** Do front-month and back-month positioning tell the same story, or are they in conflict?
3. **IV direction confirmation:** Is implied volatility actually moving in the direction your scenario requires?
4. **Shadow Greek interference:** Could complex multi-leg structures be generating flows that overwrite your schematic expectation?
5. **Centroid location:** Where is the weighted center of dealer exposure relative to current spot?

If you cannot answer these questions with reasonable confidence, reduce position size proportionally.

## 5.4 Vanna Reversal Dynamics

Vanna links volatility changes to directional hedge pressure. When vol compresses in the right context, dealers can become longer delta and may sell futures into strength; when vol expands abruptly, the opposite can trigger chase behavior.

Alma's short-vanna commentary highlights the danger: markets can look calm while structural pressure is building beneath the surface. The reversal often appears sudden only because traders were watching price and not vol-state transitions. Vanna is less about prediction and more about conditional response curves. You are not forecasting the future; you are mapping what flow must do if vol shifts.

Consider the mechanics: vanna describes how delta changes with volatility. The direction of this effect depends on whether dealers are net long or short vanna in the relevant zone, and whether implied volatility is rising or falling. If the street is net short vanna (as in many equity index environments), a fall in IV can push dealers longer delta and create selling pressure, while a rise in IV can push them shorter delta and create buying pressure. But this relationship varies with moneyness and tenor—OTM puts carry different vanna than OTM calls, and near-dated options behave differently from back-months. The key is conditional mapping: identify the dominant dealer positioning, then trace how a vol shift would force re-hedging.

The vanna regime shift is particularly problematic because it changes the market's response function. In a supportive vanna environment, falling vol helps rallies. In a suppressive vanna environment, falling vol hinders rallies. Traders who miss this distinction find themselves fighting mechanical flows they do not understand.

Research on the Vanna-Volga method (Perederiy, 2018) demonstrates how vanna exposures are used in practice for volatility smile construction and hedging in FX markets.^[arXiv:1810.07457v3] The method relies on the same principle: vanna connects volatility changes to delta adjustments, creating measurable flow effects that sophisticated traders exploit.

**Key Insight:** Vanna translates volatility state changes into directional hedge demand.

**Trading Implication:** Track vol trend and term structure with price; when both align against your setup, de-risk early rather than argue with mechanics. Never risk more than you can afford to lose in a single vanna-driven reversal, and always identify the IV level that would flip your directional assumption.

### The Vanna-Volga Interaction

In practice, vanna does not operate in isolation. It interacts with volga (vega's sensitivity to volatility changes, also called vomma) to create complex feedback loops. When dealers are short vanna and short volga simultaneously—a common configuration after periods of vol selling—rising IV creates a compounding effect. The short vanna forces dealers shorter delta as vol rises, requiring futures sales. The short volga means that vega itself increases as vol rises, amplifying the sensitivity to further vol changes.

This is why Alma emphasizes vomma supply in his August analysis. Heavy vomma supply on the right tail means dealers are selling volatility-of-volatility exposure, dampening the feedback loop. But when that vomma supply is exhausted or withdrawn, the underlying short vanna exposure remains, and the market can shift abruptly from dampened to amplified responses.

Understanding this interaction is critical for timing. A market with heavy vomma supply can absorb vol shocks that would break a market without that buffer. But the buffer is finite. When it depletes, the transition can be sudden.

## 5.5 Case Study: Vanna Regime Shift Risk (July 1, 2025)

The July 1, 2025 session illustrates how vanna regime shifts create trading risk even when spot appears stable. Alma flagged this dynamic ahead of the tariffs deadline, noting that positioning was vulnerable to a volatility-driven reversal.

As Alma wrote: "Before jumping into the intraday positioning breakdown, let me tell you why the vanna regime shift is problematic."^[Source: Alma, "The risk of vanna regime shift, Trades, Tariffs deadline" (Substack), bible/corpus/substack/unknown-date-the-risk-of-vanna-regime-shift-trades-tariffs-deadline-intra.md] The market had been in a vol-selling environment, but the transition window—where vanna dynamics could flip—was approaching.

Alma's response to this regime uncertainty was instructive: "At these points I use the spot to cash out of the positions (short vol, long delta) and roll up a small % of them only. I don't open new longs, and buy very cheap July/August OTM put spreads (30/10 deltas are my targets now), bcs reversion model says 80%+, and I want to hedge this out… other conditions are not given yet for a net short bias."

This is conditional trading in practice. Rather than predicting whether the regime shift would occur, Alma reduced exposure to the uncertain zone, took profits on positions that had worked, and added cheap convexity (long OTM put spreads) to protect against the reversion his model flagged as highly probable. His risk target remained August—the pivot point when the market would reveal whether it faced bear market conditions or mere stagnation.

**Lessons from This Case:**

**Regime shifts are timing-uncertain.** Knowing that vanna dynamics can flip is not the same as knowing when they will flip. Position for the possibility, not the certainty. The market can remain in an unstable equilibrium longer than your patience or capital can withstand.

**Reduce exposure at uncertainty nodes.** When regime shift risk elevates, the correct response is often smaller position size and wider invalidation levels, not larger bets. This is counterintuitive to the human desire for certainty, but it is what preserves capital for the actual opportunity.

**Cheap convexity as regime insurance.** OTM put spreads (or call spreads in inverse setups) provide defined-risk exposure to regime shifts without requiring precise timing. You do not need to know exactly when the shift will occur—only that the probability is elevated and the cost of protection is reasonable.

**Let the model guide, not dictate.** Alma's reversion model signaled 80%+ probability, but he still hedged rather than going all-in short. Models inform; they do not replace risk management. The best model in the world cannot protect you from a position sized so large that normal volatility forces liquidation.

**Key Insight:** Vanna regime shifts are invisible on price charts but visible in vol-state and positioning data. The trader who sees the shift coming can exit before the crowd recognizes the change.

**Trading Implication:** When your positioning model flags elevated regime-shift risk, treat it as a mandatory de-risking signal. Reduce size, widen stops, and add convexity. Assume the shift can happen faster than you can react.

## 5.6 Case Study: Supportive vs. Suppressive OpEx Flows (September 18, 2025)

The September 18, 2025 OpEx session demonstrates why context determines whether vanna and charm flows support or suppress price movement. Alma used this session to illustrate the limitations of schematic modeling and the importance of conditioning.

Alma's central question: "Supportive vanna & charm flows and so on… Is it really this simple?"

His answer: "If something is supportive or suppressive is very much context dependent, and assuming flows by using only the OTM 'skew' contracts for schematic examples is not necessarily accurate."^[Source: Alma, "OpEx Flows, supportive or suppressive vanna/charm?" (Substack), bible/corpus/substack/unknown-date-opex-flows-supportive-or-suppressive-vanna-charm-intraday-po.md]

The schematic example Alma provides is the negative speed profile: market makers net long OTM puts and net short OTM calls. In this simplified model, the result is net positive charm (because OTM puts have positive charm, and shorting OTM calls also creates positive charm exposure) and net negative vanna. The positioning could be called "right-tailish" in that it sells the left tail of the curve.

But Alma immediately qualifies this: "…even tho vanna cannot be handled this way… Is it really suppressive?" The schematic suggests that if vol is sold, negative vanna would make the dealer longer delta, forcing futures sales—while long charm also makes the dealer longer delta as time passes, adding another layer of selling pressure. This setup "tends to generate liquidity to the downside" while "preventing downside vol squeezes" and "opening the gate for upward gamma squeezes."

The critical questions that schematic modeling cannot answer:

Where is the centroid of this setup that activates one scenario over another? How do vanna, gamma, and charm interfere with time and volatility? What if the dealer doesn't simply hold these two contracts but thousands, if not millions, with different weights across the chain and tenors?

**The Conditioning Principle:**

Supportive or suppressive flows come from *passive* flows, but these can be overwritten by shadow Greek-generated flows. The net exposure on a speed profile is not a straight indicator for judging vanna and charm flows because weights differ across the surface.

**Trading Application:**

When approaching OpEx week, ask four questions:

First, what is the schematic positioning (the simple model)? This gives you a baseline hypothesis.

Second, what could overwrite or interfere with those passive flows? Shadow Greeks, cross-asset hedging, and event risk can all override mechanical expectations.

Third, where is spot relative to the centroid of dealer exposure? The same positioning creates different flows depending on whether spot is near or far from strike concentrations.

Fourth, how does the full surface (not just skew contracts) weight vanna and charm? Front-month exposure behaves differently from back-month, and OTM puts carry different Greeks than OTM calls.

**Key Insight:** OpEx flows are not deterministic. They are conditional on the full positioning surface, vol direction, and where spot trades relative to key strike concentrations.

**Trading Implication:** Use OpEx positioning as a scenario modifier, not a standalone predictor. Size positions knowing that shadow Greeks can overwrite schematic expectations, and always have a plan for when the flow goes the opposite direction.

## 5.7 Volatility Impact on Delta Exposure

Implied volatility is not a side variable; it is part of directional plumbing. In Alma's August and October notes, the repeated theme is underpricing versus reality and the risk of delayed repricing.

Falling IV can support grind-up behavior while masking fragility, especially when realized vol of vol is being aggressively sold. Rising IV can expose thin positioning and force re-hedging loops that look like sentiment shocks but are primarily mechanical. This is why delta interpretation without IV context is incomplete. Spot direction is the visible layer; IV direction often decides whether the move persists.

The August 29, 2025 analysis illustrates this dynamic clearly. Vomma supply—volatility of volatility exposure that amplifies or dampens vega sensitivity—was heavy, especially on the right-tail, because spot/vol beta was elevated at 21.01% against an average of 16.24%. Three-month realized volatility was at 9.38% while one-month RV was at 10.11%, and realized vol-of-vol (the volatility of realized volatility itself) was sold from 81.3% to 26.59% in only one week. This aggressive "higher for longer" vol environment made quant funds scale out of long exposures, decreasing institutional buying flow.^[Source: Alma, "Vomma supply, Short vanna, Risk" (Substack), bible/corpus/substack/unknown-date-vomma-supply-short-vanna-risk-intraday-post-29-aug.md]

The secondary effect is cooling down quant algos that take the velocity of the momentum into account, so they measure lower risk. If volatility were sold, the market would no longer have this braking effect. The current levels were not stable and would be held only until the September cut, simultaneously compressing risk premium.

Research on volatility swap pricing (Rolloos, 2020) confirms that zero vanna implied volatility provides important information about volatility dynamics and hedging strategies.^[arXiv:2001.02404v4] The interaction between vanna exposures and volatility state transitions creates measurable effects that sophisticated traders monitor.

**Key Insight:** Delta pressure is state-dependent on IV, not only on spot level.

**Trading Implication:** Pair directional bias with IV regime checks; if IV trend contradicts your thesis, reduce conviction and tighten risk horizons. Never let a position grow large enough that an IV spike could force liquidation—size for the vol regime, not just the price target.

## 5.8 The JPM Collar: Context, Not Causation

The JPM collar is useful as a teaching lens, but Alma repeatedly warns against mythology. It can influence local flow and vol perception, especially around rebalance windows, yet it is one structure in a wider inventory ecosystem. Treating it as the master key usually creates overfit narratives.

The better approach is conditional: ask where spot sits versus key strikes, what vol is doing, and whether broader dealer inventory reinforces or offsets the collar footprint. Collar effects are often path dependent and timing sensitive. That is why serious traders map context first, then collar impact.

In the June 30, 2025 analysis, Alma explicitly downplayed collar obsession: "Don't expect too much impact from the JPM collar reset. It has more local impact, and vol impact on the long run, if spot is in a certain area. But it is only one trade among many others, and this time it has minor impact. Don't fall for furus who try to sound clever through explaining the collar over and over again, and mostly wrongly."^[Source: Alma, "Theta decay, 'when will vol come back?'" (Substack), bible/corpus/substack/unknown-date-theta-decay-when-will-vol-come-back-intraday-post-30-june-wi.md]

The collar's influence waxes and wanes with market conditions. During periods of elevated uncertainty, its hedging activity may amplify local volatility dynamics. During calm regimes, it may be absorbed into broader flow without noticeable impact. The error is treating any single structure as deterministic when it operates within a complex adaptive system.

The March 24-28, 2025 weekly post provides additional context on how the collar actually functions. Alma explains that the JPM hedge equity operation consists of three funds, with JHEQX being the largest. These funds operate continuously with overlapping expirations. Unlike a traditional protective collar, the fund uses a debit spread: "The extra premium gained from selling the put option lets the manager raise the call strike and increase the cap. This spread protects against losses beyond roughly 5%, but after about 20% losses, the protection ends and performance tracks the market one-to-one. It does not offer coverage against extreme tail events."^[Source: Alma, "Weekly post (24-28/march)" (Substack), bible/corpus/substack/unknown-date-weekly-post-24-28-march.md]

Understanding this structure matters for timing. Because they use SPXW options (EOM, PM-settled), execution and resetting are tied to the closing settlement. This creates predictable windows of potential flow, but the direction and magnitude depend on where spot trades relative to the spread strikes and what the broader vol environment is doing.

**Key Insight:** The collar is an influence node, not a complete market model.

**Trading Implication:** Use collar windows as scenario modifiers, not standalone directional triggers. When every furu is talking about the collar, the edge is likely gone—focus on what they are missing instead. Size positions assuming the collar effect will be smaller than advertised, and define the spot level where your collar-based thesis would be proven wrong.

## 5.9 Speed Profile in Practice (and How to Avoid the Common Failure Mode)

The common failure mode is simple: traders learn the vocabulary (vanna, gamma, skew, speed) and then use it to justify conviction.

That is the exact opposite of what these tools are for.

Vanna and speed should reduce your certainty, not increase it. They exist to remind you that:

- The chain is uneven across strikes and tenors.
- The weights are not constant.
- The sign of the flow can invert if IV direction changes.

This is why Alma repeatedly frames supportive/suppressive flows as context dependent. When traders model only a couple of skew contracts, they produce stories that feel clean and are often wrong, because the book is a weighted surface, not a cartoon.

If you want a practical way to use speed without turning it into mythology, use it to ask three questions:

First, if spot moves, does gamma *concentrate* (speed adverse) or *dilute* (speed favorable)? This tells you whether the market's response to price movement will accelerate or dampen.

Second, if IV moves, does delta pressure become more stabilizing (vanna supportive) or more destabilizing (vanna suppressive)? This tells you whether volatility expansion will help or hurt your directional thesis.

Third, where is this true: front-end only, or across multiple tenors? Front-heavy effects are more immediate but shorter-lived. Back-month effects take longer to develop but can be more persistent.

You are not trying to guess the next candle. You are trying to map the response function of the tape.

**Trading Implication:** Use speed and vanna to build conditional plans (if spot moves / if IV moves), not a single-path forecast. Size every position for the possibility that your speed/vanna read is wrong, and always know the market conditions that would invalidate your scenario.

## 5.10 Worked Example: August 29 Vomma Supply and Short Vanna Risk

To make these concepts concrete, let us walk through the August 29, 2025 session Alma analyzed. This example demonstrates how vanna, vomma, and vol-of-vol interact to create a specific trading environment.

**The Setup:**

Alma observed that "vomma supply is on (grey line), and very heavy, as we keep seeing it day by day, week by week: especially on the right-tail, because spot/vol beta is about 21.01% with an avg of 16.24%."^[Source: Alma, "Vomma supply, Short vanna, Risk" (Substack), bible/corpus/substack/unknown-date-vomma-supply-short-vanna-risk-intraday-post-29-aug.md]

**Key Metrics:**
- Spot/vol beta: 21.01% (vs. 16.24% average)
- 3-month realized volatility: 9.38%
- 1-month realized volatility: 10.11%
- Realized vol-of-vol: Sold from 81.3% to 26.59% in one week

**The Interpretation:**

The elevated spot/vol beta meant that volatility changes were creating larger delta adjustments than normal. With vomma supply concentrated on the right tail, dealers were actively selling volatility-of-volatility exposure, creating a "braking effect" on upside momentum. This allowed funds to extend the melt-up while keeping volatility cheap on the downside for future correction hedging.

The aggressive "higher for longer" vol environment forced quant funds to scale out of long exposures, decreasing institutional buying flow. This was not a sentiment shift per se—it was a mechanical response to the vol environment.

**The Secondary Effect:**

Quant algos that incorporate momentum velocity into their risk models measured lower risk due to the vol suppression. If volatility were sold (rather than supplied), this braking effect would disappear, potentially unleashing sharper moves.

**The Dynamic Interplay:**

Notice how multiple second-order effects compound in this scenario. The vomma supply (vol of vol selling) dampens vega sensitivity changes, making the environment feel stable. The short vanna means that any IV expansion would force dealers shorter delta, potentially accelerating any downside move. The elevated spot/vol beta means that small vol changes create larger delta adjustments than normal. These are not independent variables—they interact multiplicatively.

This is why Alma emphasizes the danger of "higher for longer" vol environments. They appear stable on the surface while building structural fragility underneath. The quant funds scaling out of long exposures are not making sentiment-based decisions; they are responding to mechanically deteriorating risk-adjusted returns. The algos measuring lower risk are not wrong in their calculation—they are simply responding to the suppressed vol-of-vol signal, even as the underlying positioning becomes more vulnerable.

**Trading Application:**

In this environment, a trader might:

First, recognize that the "stable" grind-up was actually a vol-suppressed, mechanically constrained move—not a sign of fundamental strength.

Second, understand that the low realized vol-of-vol was temporary (sold down from 81% to 26% in a week) and therefore unlikely to persist.

Third, position for the eventual vol expansion rather than chasing the grind. This means avoiding new longs at elevated levels and preparing for regime shift.

Fourth, use the cheap downside vol (created by right-tail vomma supply) to hedge long exposure through OTM put spreads or similar structures.

Fifth, size positions for the possibility of a rapid regime shift when the vol suppression ends. This means smaller position sizes, wider stops, and explicit invalidation levels.

**Risk Management Framework:**

In the August 29 environment, prudent risk management would include:

- **Position sizing:** Reduce gross exposure and shorten time horizons relative to normal vol environments. The suppressed volatility creates false confidence; size for the regime that will exist, not the one that currently exists. *(Example for illustration only—not a recommendation: a trader might consider reducing long delta exposure significantly, perhaps by 25-50% or more depending on account size and risk tolerance, but this range is hypothetical and must be adapted to individual circumstances.)*

- **Hedge ratio:** Increase put/call hedge ratio to account for the asymmetric risk profile. The downside is underpriced relative to the upside.

- **Stop discipline:** Tighten stops on long positions because the "stable" environment could flip quickly. What looks like support may evaporate when mechanical suppression ends.

- **Vol trigger:** Pre-define the VIX or IV level that would signal the regime shift has begun. Do not wait for confirmation from price action alone.

- **Time horizon:** Shorten holding periods for directional trades because the mechanical suppression has a limited lifespan (in this case, until the September cut).

**Key Insight:** High vomma supply with short vanna risk creates an asymmetric environment where upside is dampened but downside convexity remains cheap. The stability is temporary, and when it ends, the move can be violent because the suppressed vol-of-vol has allowed positioning to become one-sided.

**Trading Implication:** When you see vol-of-vol aggressively sold while spot/vol beta is elevated, assume the calm is mechanical, not fundamental. Size for vol expansion, not vol compression, and use the cheap downside to hedge. Define the IV level where your thesis activates, and be prepared to act before the crowd recognizes the regime change. Never assume the suppression will last indefinitely—mechanical reversion is inevitable.

## Mini-Glossary

**Vomma:** The second derivative of option value with respect to volatility (d²V/dσ²). Measures how vega itself changes as implied volatility moves—high vomma exposure means volatility changes create accelerating hedge adjustments.

**Realized vol-of-vol:** The volatility of realized volatility; quantifies how much the actual measured volatility of an asset fluctuates over time. When aggressively sold, it indicates market participants are betting on stable volatility conditions.

**Shadow Greeks:** Greeks not directly visible in standard positioning summaries but generated by complex multi-leg structures or cross-tenor interactions. These can overwrite or interfere with passive flow expectations.

**Centroid:** The weighted center of dealer exposure across the volatility surface. The location of the centroid relative to current spot determines which flow scenario (supportive or suppressive) is likely to dominate.

**Spot/vol beta:** The sensitivity of spot price movements to changes in implied volatility. Elevated spot/vol beta means volatility changes create larger delta adjustments than normal.
