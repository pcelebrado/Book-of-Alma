## Opening Hook

Volatility becomes a regime only when a macro story gives fear a memory.

A volatility spike can be mechanical—a gamma squeeze, a liquidation cascade, a dealer hedge. But a volatility *regime* requires something more durable. It needs narrative continuity. Once market participants can explain risk in a coherent storyline, they keep repricing that risk beyond the first shock. The story does not have to be objectively complete. It only has to be behaviorally sticky.

This chapter maps how geopolitical narratives anchor volatility persistence and how the fragmentation of the global order reshapes liquidity, pricing, and the very structure of risk. The mechanics matter, but the story matters more.

## 8.1 Volatility Requires Narrative

A volatility spike can be mechanical, but a volatility regime needs narrative continuity.

Consider the difference between an April 2025 tariff announcement and the subsequent months of trade-war positioning. The initial shock—Trump's reciprocal tariff notifications—produced a mechanical repricing. SPX dropped, VIX spiked, dealers hedged. But the *regime* that followed was not maintained by the tariff levels themselves. It was maintained by the story: that the U.S. and China were engaged in a prolonged confrontation, that Europe faced stagflationary pressure, that the post-Cold War economic order was fragmenting faster than policymakers could adapt.

Alma's geopolitical framing in April 2025 captures this precisely: "The global economy—and therefore the entire market structure—is undergoing a profound transformation, and every market reaction today is driven first and foremost by the headlines"[^1]. The mechanical shock passed in days. The narrative regime persisted for months.

The psychological mechanism is straightforward. When participants can explain price action through a coherent macro story, they extend their time horizon. Short-term mean-reversion reflexes get overridden by medium-term repositioning. The narrative provides *permission* to stay positioned for volatility. Without that permission, fear mean-reverts. With it, fear compounds into positioning.

This is why Alma emphasizes that "investors simply aren't buying the narrative right now: they react to concrete actions, not tweets or promises"[^1]. Narrative coherence is not about truth—it is about behavioral stickiness. A sticky narrative creates persistent repositioning. Persistent repositioning creates regime-level volatility.

**Key Insight:** Narrative coherence is a persistence engine for volatility.

**Trading Implication:** When vol rises with a coherent macro story, extend horizon assumptions and avoid quick mean-reversion reflexes. The mechanical spike may fade, but the narrative regime can sustain elevated volatility long after the initial shock.

## 8.2 Fragmentation and Supply Chain Inflation

Geopolitical fragmentation does not only change trade routes. It changes cost structures, lead times, and pricing confidence.

The shift from offshore to "friend-shore" production is not a frictionless reallocation. It embeds persistent cost pressures into supply chains that were optimized for a different geopolitical era. Alma's stagflation notes repeatedly connect this fragmentation to inflation persistence: "The eurozone is headed for stagflation"[^1], with input costs rising even as growth slows.

The ISM Manufacturing Prices Paid Index returned to expansion in October 2024 near 55% and climbed to 69.8% by April 2025—its highest since June 2022. The Services equivalent jumped from 58% to 65.1%, its strongest pace since January 2023. Both surveys spent seven consecutive months above 60%, indicating sustained price pressures. Five of the six largest manufacturing industries reported higher raw-material costs. In services, only arts and recreation reported lower input costs; all others paid more[^2].

This is not a temporary bottleneck. It is a structural repricing of global production. When supply chains fragment, redundancy replaces efficiency. Redundancy costs more. Those costs pass through to consumers, or they compress margins, or both. Either way, the inflationary pressure persists even when demand softens.

Alma's framework captures this duality: markets can rally on short-term liquidity while embedding medium-term cost instability. The April 2025 period demonstrated this perfectly—equities bounced on relief flows even as tariff-driven inflationary pressures continued building in the pipeline. That duality is why regime calls fail when they focus on one horizon only.

The fragmentation also creates asymmetric inflation risk. Supply shocks can push prices higher even when demand is weak. This is the stagflationary core: not just high inflation, not just low growth, but both simultaneously, feeding on each other through expectations and wage responses.

**Key Insight:** Geopolitical fragmentation embeds inflation asymmetry into otherwise risk-on tapes.

**Trading Implication:** Treat supply-chain and tariff signals as regime modifiers, not as one-day headline noise. The inflationary pressure from fragmentation operates on a different timescale than the equity market's liquidity-driven rallies.

## 8.3 De-Dollarization Mechanics

Reserve composition and trade-settlement shifts *can* alter funding behavior before they appear in broad narratives—*if* they occur at sufficient scale.

De-dollarization is often discussed in apocalyptic terms—the dollar's imminent collapse, the yuan's inevitable rise. Alma's macro lens treats it differently: as a *potential* gradual marginal shift that *could* rewire demand for duration, collateral, and cross-currency hedging long before consensus narratives catch up.

Consider the mechanics: if the U.S. dollar were to fall to multi-year lows amid cooling inflation prints and tariff relief hopes, the driver might not be just relative growth differentials. It could reflect a repricing of the dollar's structural role as global funding currency. If foreign investors hedge long-USD exposures more aggressively—*if* FX hedging costs were to fall—this could add pressure to the dollar even while U.S. rates remain relatively high.

These shifts *would* affect volatility structure in subtle but important ways. *If* foreign central banks were to diversify away from Treasuries, term premium would likely rise to attract domestic buyers. Rising term premium would increase discount-rate volatility, which would feed through to equity valuations.

The key observation is that currency regime drift *can* affect funding and volatility channels before consensus narratives catch up. The market may reprice plumbing first, story second. If de-dollarization pressures were to build, rates and FX markets would likely absorb and distribute that pressure before it appears in mainstream financial media.

The mechanics would operate through multiple channels: reduced foreign demand for U.S. duration, shifts in commodity pricing currencies, changes in swap market dynamics. Each of these *could* alter the volatility surface in ways that equity-only traders might miss until correlation shifts become undeniable.

**Observable Proxies:**
- DXY (U.S. Dollar Index) weakness despite positive rate differentials
- Rising 10-year Treasury term premium (available from Fed data)
- Increased FX hedging activity by foreign Treasury holders
- Shifts in commodity pricing away from dollar denominations

**Key Insight:** Currency regime drift *can* affect funding and volatility channels before consensus narratives catch up—but only when observable proxies confirm the shift is occurring.

**Trading Implication:** Monitor rates/FX stress signals as early warnings for equity-regime instability. When the dollar weakens despite rate differentials, or when Treasury term premium rises without corresponding inflation shocks, these are signals that the funding plumbing may be shifting beneath the surface.

## 8.4 Regime Transition Dynamics

Regime transitions are rarely clean. They pass through overlap periods where old playbooks partially work and then abruptly fail.

Alma's regime language emphasizes this ambiguity: traders must carry conditional maps rather than single-path conviction. During transition, valuation anchors move, liquidity quality changes, and volatility elasticity becomes unstable. The winning posture is adaptive, not declarative.

The April–June 2025 period exemplified this transition. Initially, the market treated tariff announcements as negotiable—temporary disruptions that would resolve through deal-making. By May, the narrative shifted to stagflationary persistence. By June, the focus moved to Fed response functions and hidden liquidity support. Each phase required different positioning, and each phase invalidated the prior framework.

Alma's window-of-risk framework captures this progression: "My primal window of risk was the May ECB rate decision to mid-May... Later, a 124 pts decline materialized after Trump's visit... The next, and basically my original major window of risk was the July/August window." These were not predictions of specific events. They were mappings of regime vulnerability—periods when the transition dynamics made standard positioning especially dangerous.

During transition, the stock-bond correlation can flip abruptly. Alma notes that "equity–yields correlation has jumped to ~70 bps over the past week (from -22% to 52%), signaling that inflation surprises have become the primary driver of both yields and equity returns"[^3]. This abrupt flip illustrates a broader pattern: when inflation becomes sufficiently volatile, correlations that were negative during the low-inflation regime can flip positive—and diversification benefits that traders relied upon may suddenly disappear.

The danger is not being wrong about direction. The danger is applying a static framework to a dynamic transition. When correlations shift, hedges fail. When volatility elasticity changes, position sizing assumptions break. When liquidity quality deteriorates, exit strategies that worked in the prior regime become traps.

**Key Insight:** Transition regimes punish static frameworks more than outright wrong forecasts.

**Trading Implication:** Use staged scenario trees with explicit invalidation, and rebalance risk when transition evidence accumulates. Do not commit fully to a single regime call until the transition has clearly resolved. Carry optionality through the ambiguity.

## 8.5 A Taxonomy of Geopolitical-Volatility Regimes

Not all geopolitical stress produces the same volatility signature. The interaction between political narrative, economic structure, and market positioning creates distinct regime types, each with its own risk profile and trading implications. Understanding these regime archetypes allows traders to match positioning to conditions rather than applying a one-size-fits-all volatility playbook.

### Regime Type I: The Escalation Cascade

**What it looks like:** A series of discrete political events that compound market uncertainty through narrative momentum rather than immediate economic impact. Trade announcements, sanctions, diplomatic withdrawals, or military posturing create a drumbeat of headlines that keeps risk premia elevated. Each individual event may be absorbable; the sequence becomes destabilizing.

The April–May 2025 period exemplified this regime. Trump's tariff notifications, China's retaliatory measures, European positioning responses, and ongoing Middle East tensions created overlapping uncertainty layers. Alma captured this dynamic: "investors simply aren't buying the narrative right now: they react to concrete actions, not tweets or promises"[^1]. The regime persisted not because any single tariff rate was economically catastrophic, but because the sequence suggested an open-ended confrontation without clear de-escalation pathways.

**What works:**
- Long volatility positions with extended time horizons (avoiding short-dated theta decay during prolonged uncertainty)
- Correlation-aware hedging that assumes equity-bond diversification may fail
- Staged entry strategies that scale into positions as narrative coherence builds
- Focus on second-order effects (supply chain disruptions, regional growth impacts) rather than headline trading

**What fails:**
- Quick mean-reversion plays based on "diplomatic optimism" headlines
- Static hedges that assume normal correlation structures
- Binary event positioning that treats each announcement as a one-day trade
- Overreliance on historical volatility distributions during narrative momentum phases

**Risk:** The escalation cascade regime can persist longer than positioning patience. Traders who correctly identify the regime may still lose on timing if they size for quick resolution while the narrative continues building.

### Regime Type II: The Stagflationary Squeeze

**What it looks like:** Geopolitical fragmentation combines with supply constraints to produce simultaneous growth slowdown and inflation persistence. This is not a standard recession (falling growth, falling inflation) or a standard expansion (rising growth, rising inflation). It is the worst of both worlds: rising input costs, falling output, and policy paralysis as central banks face impossible trade-offs.

Alma's May 2025 analysis identified this regime clearly: "The eurozone is headed for stagflation"[^1], with input costs rising even as growth indicators softened. The ISM data confirmed this—prices paid indices climbing above 60% while new orders and employment components showed deceleration. This is the fragmentation premium in action: supply chains reorganize around geopolitical fault lines, embedding cost increases that monetary policy cannot easily address.

The stagflationary squeeze has distinct volatility characteristics. Equity-bond correlations flip positive as inflation becomes the dominant driver of both asset classes. Real yields become more volatile as markets price competing scenarios of central bank response. Credit spreads widen not just on growth concerns but on the specific risk that inflation will force tighter policy even as growth slows.

**What works:**
- Long real rate volatility (inflation-linked bond options, yield curve steepeners)
- Commodity exposure as both inflation hedge and supply constraint beneficiary
- Equity factor rotation toward pricing power (firms that can pass costs through)
- Explicit correlation-hedge overlays that assume positive stock-bond covariance

**What fails:**
- Traditional 60/40 portfolio construction (bonds no longer hedge equity risk)
- Pure growth or pure value tilts (regime affects both through different channels)
- Duration-heavy fixed income (inflation volatility dominates rate expectations)
- Standard recession plays that assume deflationary pressure

**Risk:** The stagflationary squeeze can trigger policy errors. Central banks that prioritize inflation control deepen the growth slowdown; those that prioritize growth allow inflation expectations to de-anchor. Both paths produce volatility, but the timing and distribution differ unpredictably.

### Regime Type III: The Liquidity Fracture

**What it looks like:** Geopolitical stress manifests not through gradual repricing but through sudden liquidity withdrawals in specific funding markets. This is the most dangerous regime because it can emerge with little warning and propagate through channels that standard risk models miss. Currency markets seize. Cross-border funding becomes intermittent. Safe-haven flows concentrate in unexpected instruments while traditional havens underperform.

Alma's liquidity crisis framing from May 2025 captures the mechanics: "Heading into a liquidity crisis"[^2] with specific attention to how input-cost inflation and margin squeeze create funding stress that precedes broader market dislocation. The liquidity fracture regime often begins in peripheral markets—emerging market debt, regional banking systems, commodity-dependent currencies—before propagating to core markets.

The key signature is divergence: safe-haven currencies that normally correlate (USD, JPY, CHF) begin moving independently. Treasury yields may fall (flight to quality) while credit spreads widen (funding stress), but the relationship becomes unstable. Gold may rally not as inflation hedge but as settlement-medium alternative. Bitcoin and crypto assets may decouple from equity beta as they become vehicles for capital flight in specific jurisdictions.

**What works:**
- Maximum liquidity preference (cash, short-duration Treasuries, repo markets)
- Options-based hedges that benefit from convexity rather than delta positioning
- Geographic diversification that assumes regional decoupling
- Explicit funding-cost monitoring as early warning system

**What fails:**
- Leveraged carry trades that assume continuous funding availability
- Complex multi-leg strategies that require stable liquidity for adjustment
- Correlation-based risk parity (assumptions break during fracture)
- Illiquid alternative assets that cannot be monetized during stress

**Risk:** Liquidity fracture regimes produce gap risk that cannot be hedged with standard instruments. Stop-losses fail to execute. Option spreads blow out. The very act of seeking liquidity can accelerate the fracture for others, creating feedback loops that standard models underestimate.

### Regime Boundaries and Overlap

These three regime types rarely exist in pure form. More commonly, markets transition through overlap periods where characteristics of multiple regimes coexist. The April–June 2025 period demonstrated this clearly: the initial tariff shock created Escalation Cascade dynamics, which evolved into Stagflationary Squeeze characteristics as input cost data accumulated, with periodic Liquidity Fracture signals in specific funding markets.

Understanding regime boundaries matters for position sizing. When regimes overlap, standard position-sizing assumptions break down because volatility of volatility increases. A position sized for Escalation Cascade volatility may be dangerously large if Liquidity Fracture characteristics emerge. This is why the proxy checklist in Section 8.6 emphasizes multiple confirmation thresholds rather than single-signal regime calls.

Regime transitions often produce the most dangerous trading conditions. Old playbooks partially work, creating false confidence, then abruptly fail when the new regime fully asserts. The trader's job is not to predict the exact timing of regime shift but to recognize when overlap conditions increase the cost of being wrong—and to size positions accordingly.

**Key Insight:** Regime overlap periods demand reduced position sizing even when directional conviction is high. The volatility of regime transition itself is a risk factor that standard models underweight.

**Trading Implication:** When proxy indicators suggest multiple regime types may be active simultaneously, reduce gross exposure and increase explicit hedge ratios. The cost of over-hedging during overlap is smaller than the cost of under-hedging during regime consolidation.

## 8.6 The Proxy Checklist: Monitoring Regime Persistence

Geopolitical-volatility regimes do not announce their arrival or departure with clarity. They emerge through gradual shifts in observable proxies that traders can monitor systematically. The following checklist provides a framework for tracking regime persistence across rates, FX, commodities, term structure, and correlation domains.

### Rates and Fixed Income Proxies

**10-Year Treasury Term Premium**
- *What to watch:* Fed data on term premium decomposition (available from Adrian-Crump-Moench models)
- *Regime signal:* Rising term premium without corresponding inflation shock suggests foreign demand erosion or duration risk repricing
- *Persistence threshold:* Three consecutive months of rising term premium above 50bps
- *Invalidation:* Term premium compression concurrent with equity rally suggests regime exhaustion

**Real Yield Volatility**
- *What to watch:* 10-year TIPS yield daily volatility and options-implied volatility on inflation-linked bonds
- *Regime signal:* Real yield vol above historical 90th percentile indicates inflation uncertainty dominance
- *Persistence threshold:* Sustained elevation (>20 trading days) above 90th percentile
- *Invalidation:* Real yield vol normalization while nominal vol remains elevated suggests pure inflation risk has passed

**Credit Spread Term Structure**
- *What to watch:* IG and HY credit spreads across 1-year, 5-year, and 10-year maturities
- *Regime signal:* Steepening spread curve (near-term spreads widening faster than long-term) indicates funding stress
- *Persistence threshold:* Spread curve inversion or extreme steepening persisting >15 trading days
- *Invalidation:* Parallel spread compression across maturities suggests systemic risk reduction

### FX and Currency Proxies

**DXY Divergence from Rate Differentials**
- *What to watch:* DXY level versus weighted U.S. rate differential to G10 currencies
- *Regime signal:* DXY weakness despite positive or stable rate differentials suggests structural dollar demand erosion
- *Persistence threshold:* 30+ trading days of negative correlation between DXY and rate spreads
- *Invalidation:* DXY re-coupling to rate differentials indicates cyclical factors reasserting over structural

**FX Hedging Cost Trends**
- *What to watch:* Cross-currency basis swaps (EUR/USD, USD/JPY) and implied hedging costs for foreign Treasury holders
- *Regime signal:* Falling hedging costs concurrent with Treasury outflows suggests foreign repositioning
- *Persistence threshold:* Basis swap normalization while foreign holdings decline
- *Invalidation:* Rising hedging costs with renewed foreign buying indicates temporary repositioning only

**Commodity Currency Divergence**
- *What to watch:* AUD, CAD, NOK performance versus commodity price indices
- *Regime signal:* Commodity currencies underperforming commodity prices suggests growth concern override
- *Persistence threshold:* 20+ trading days of negative correlation between commodity prices and commodity FX
- *Invalidation:* Re-coupling suggests growth concerns have been priced or resolved

### Commodity and Real Asset Proxies

**Supply Chain Stress Indicators**
- *What to watch:* Baltic Dry Index, container shipping rates, ISM delivery times, inventory-to-sales ratios
- *Regime signal:* Rising shipping costs and extended delivery times concurrent with inventory accumulation
- *Persistence threshold:* Three consecutive months of elevated readings
- *Invalidation:* Shipping cost normalization while input prices remain elevated suggests supply constraints easing

**Commodity Term Structure Shape**
- *What to watch:* Crude oil, copper, and grain futures curves (front-month versus 12-month spreads)
- *Regime signal:* Persistent backwardation in energy and industrial metals suggests immediate supply scarcity
- *Persistence threshold:* Backwardation persisting >60 days in key commodities
- *Invalidation:* Contango return suggests supply-demand balance restoration or demand collapse

**Gold versus Real Rates**
- *What to watch:* Gold price correlation to 10-year TIPS yields
- *Regime signal:* Gold rallying despite rising real yields suggests safe-haven or de-dollarization demand
- *Persistence threshold:* Positive gold-real rate correlation persisting >30 trading days
- *Invalidation:* Normal negative correlation resumption indicates traditional drivers reasserting

### Equity-Rate Correlation Proxies

**SPX-LTY Correlation**
- *What to watch:* Rolling 30-day correlation between S&P 500 returns and 10-year Treasury yield changes
- *Regime signal:* Correlation flipping from negative to positive indicates inflation-driven joint movement
- *Persistence threshold:* Positive correlation sustained >20 trading days with magnitude >0.3
- *Invalidation:* Correlation return to negative territory suggests growth-risk dominance restored

Alma's observation from May 2025 provides the template: "equity–yields correlation has jumped to ~70 bps over the past week (from -22% to 52%), signaling that inflation surprises (and hence inflation expectations) have become the primary driver of both yields and equity returns"[^3]. This flip is not merely a statistical curiosity—it is a regime marker that invalidates portfolio construction assumptions.

**Sectoral Correlation Shifts**
- *What to watch:* Correlation between rate-sensitive sectors (utilities, REITs, staples) and growth sectors (tech, discretionary)
- *Regime signal:* Historical sector correlations breaking down as inflation affects each differently
- *Persistence threshold:* Sector correlation breakdown persisting through multiple macro data releases
- *Invalidation:* Sector correlations normalizing suggests single-factor (growth or inflation) dominance

**Volatility of Volatility (Vol-of-Vol)**
- *What to watch:* VIX options implied volatility, VVIX index, and VIX term structure steepness
- *Regime signal:* Elevated vol-of-vol suggests uncertainty about uncertainty—participants cannot price risk consistently
- *Persistence threshold:* VVIX >120 for >10 trading days
- *Invalidation:* Vol-of-vol normalization while spot VIX remains elevated suggests consensus forming on risk magnitude

### Cross-Asset Correlation Proxies

**Cross-Asset Correlation Matrix Stability**
- *What to watch:* Rolling correlations between equities, bonds, commodities, and FX
- *Regime signal:* Correlation matrix instability (frequent sign changes and magnitude shifts)
- *Persistence threshold:* >50% of major asset pair correlations changing sign within 30 days
- *Invalidation:* Correlation matrix stabilization suggests regime consolidation

**Safe Haven Rotation**
- *What to watch:* Relative performance of USD, JPY, CHF, gold, and long-duration Treasuries during equity stress
- *Regime signal:* Traditional safe havens diverging (some rallying, some not) indicates funding stress
- *Persistence threshold:* Safe haven divergence persisting through multiple equity down days
- *Invalidation:* Convergent safe-haven performance suggests unified flight-to-quality

### Using the Checklist: A Disciplined Workflow

The proxy checklist is not meant to be consulted once and forgotten. It should be integrated into a regular monitoring workflow that tracks regime persistence and flags potential transitions. The following workflow provides a template for systematic application:

**Daily Review (5–10 minutes):**
- Check SPX-LTY correlation (30-day rolling)
- Note VIX level and VVIX (vol-of-vol)
- Scan DXY and major FX crosses for unusual moves
- Review commodity price action (crude, copper, gold)

**Weekly Review (30 minutes):**
- Update all correlation proxies (equity-bond, sectoral, cross-asset)
- Check term premium estimates and credit spread curves
- Review ISM and supply chain indicators
- Assess safe-haven performance during any equity stress periods

**Monthly Review (comprehensive):**
- Full proxy checklist assessment across all categories
- Regime classification based on active signals
- Position audit: are current positions aligned with identified regime?
- Invalidation review: which positions have explicit regime-based stop criteria?

The goal of this workflow is not to predict regime changes but to detect them early enough to adjust positioning before maximum damage occurs. Regime shifts are rarely one-day events; they are processes that unfold over weeks. The checklist provides the observational discipline to catch these processes in motion.

**Key Insight:** Regime monitoring is a continuous process, not a discrete decision. The checklist provides structure for this process, but the trader must supply the discipline to review it consistently.

## 8.7 Bible-Anchored Vignettes: Regime Recognition in Real Time

The following vignettes demonstrate how Alma's live market commentary identified regime characteristics as they emerged. These are not retrospective reconstructions—they are the actual analytical frames applied during the periods in question, captured in the Bible corpus.

### Vignette I: The Narrative Rejection of April 2025

**Context:** April 2025 saw a series of tariff announcements, diplomatic communications, and policy responses that created substantial headline volatility. The mechanical market response was immediate—equity selloffs, VIX spikes, sector rotations. But the regime characteristics depended on how market participants processed the narrative, not just the headlines themselves.

**Alma's Regime Identification:**

In the April 20, 2025 weekly post, Alma identified a critical regime marker that distinguished mechanical volatility from sustained regime: "investors simply aren't buying the narrative right now: they react to concrete actions, not tweets or promises"[^1]. This observation captures the essence of narrative-driven regime identification.

The specific evidence Alma cited was commodity market behavior: "You can see it in commodity markets—grain, soy, rare earths—where China is clearly signaling that it's preparing for a prolonged confrontation and ready to challenge US hegemony"[^1]. This was not abstract geopolitical analysis—it was a tradable observation about how different market segments were pricing the same narrative.

**Regime Implications:**

The regime that emerged was an Escalation Cascade (Type I) with developing Stagflationary Squeeze (Type II) characteristics. The narrative rejection meant that positive diplomatic headlines would not sustain rallies—only concrete de-escalation would. The commodity market signals suggested supply chain repositioning that would embed cost pressures regardless of short-term tariff rates.

**Trading Application:**

Alma's framework suggested avoiding mean-reversion plays on diplomatic optimism and instead positioning for prolonged uncertainty: "I've been saying since February that the bottom confirmation will only come with a cease-fire and the start of sustainable US QE, likely around June"[^1]. This was not a prediction of specific price levels—it was a regime-conditioned timeline for when narrative coherence might shift.

The vignette demonstrates how Bible-captured analysis connects headline flow to market structure through the lens of narrative credibility. Traders who tracked commodity market divergence from equity optimism had an early signal that the regime would persist beyond initial shock.

### Vignette II: The Correlation Flip of May 2025

**Context:** By mid-May 2025, inflation data and Fed policy expectations had created a shift in how equity and bond markets interacted. The traditional negative correlation—where bonds rally when equities fall, providing portfolio diversification—had begun to invert.

**Alma's Regime Identification:**

The May 13, 2025 intraday post captured this shift with quantitative precision: "equity–yields correlation has jumped to ~70 bps over the past week (from -22% to 52%), signaling that inflation surprises (and hence inflation expectations) have become the primary driver of both yields and equity returns"[^3].

Alma connected this observation to broader regime dynamics: "This abrupt flip resembles the mid-2021 shift documented by the BIS: once inflation is volatile enough, positive correlations persist and diversification benefits fade"[^3]. The citation of BIS research demonstrates how Alma anchors live observations in historical precedent, creating a framework for persistence assessment.

The mechanism Alma identified was volatility-driven: "The stock–bond correlation depends not on the level of inflation per se but on the relative volatility of growth vs. inflation surprises and their covariance. When inflation uncertainty rises, its shocks loom larger, flipping correlations positive and eroding bonds' hedge role"[^3].

**Regime Implications:**

This was a clear Stagflationary Squeeze (Type II) regime marker. The correlation flip meant that traditional portfolio hedges would fail precisely when they were most needed. The "primary driver" framing—"inflation surprises (and hence inflation expectations) have become the primary driver of both yields and equity returns"—indicated that growth dynamics had been subordinated to inflation dynamics in market pricing.

Alma extended the analysis to term premium implications: "With equities and bonds moving together, passive portfolios demand extra compensation for undiversified bond risk, pushing term premia—and long yields—higher over time"[^3]. This created a feedback loop where the correlation shift itself became a driver of further yield pressure.

**Trading Application:**

The vignette provides a template for real-time regime recognition. The correlation flip was not merely a statistical observation—it was a signal to abandon traditional hedging assumptions and adopt correlation-aware positioning. Traders who recognized this shift could avoid the trap of increasing bond allocations as equity risk rose, which would have compounded losses as both assets declined together.

The specific thresholds Alma cited—movement from -22% to +52% correlation—provide quantitative benchmarks for regime identification. Future traders can apply similar monitoring to detect correlation regime shifts as they emerge.

## Chapter Close

Geopolitical volatility is not about predicting the next headline. It is about recognizing when a narrative has achieved sufficient coherence to sustain repositioning beyond the initial shock.

The fragmentation of the global order, the shift in supply chain economics, the gradual drift in currency regimes—these are not one-day events. They are structural forces that reshape volatility persistence, correlation structures, and the very meaning of risk-on and risk-off.

Traders who focus only on the mechanical aspects—gamma levels, dealer positioning, flow imbalances—miss the regime-level dynamics that make those mechanics matter. The narrative gives fear its memory. Without the narrative, volatility mean-reverts. With it, volatility compounds into regime.

The taxonomy provided in this chapter—Escalation Cascade, Stagflationary Squeeze, Liquidity Fracture—offers a framework for matching positioning to conditions. The proxy checklist provides concrete observables for regime monitoring. The vignettes demonstrate how these frameworks emerge from live market analysis.

What remains constant across regimes is the requirement for adaptive posture. Static frameworks fail not because the analysis was wrong but because the regime shifted beneath it. The winning trader carries conditional maps, explicit invalidation criteria, and the discipline to reposition when the evidence changes.

The next chapter examines how financing mechanisms sustain overvaluation and defer repricing—how bubbles survive not on belief alone, but on the pathways that keep belief funded.
