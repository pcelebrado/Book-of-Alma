## 7.1 The Intraday Decision Loop: A Time-Anchored Framework

Professional intraday trading operates on a different clock than swing trading or investing. The day unfolds in distinct phases, each with its own mechanical characteristics, liquidity patterns, and edge opportunities. Alma's framework treats these windows not as arbitrary time divisions but as structural phases where specific types of positioning behavior become more or less probable.

### The Five-Phase Trading Day

**Phase 1: Premarket (6:00–9:30 AM ET)**

The premarket phase is for information assimilation, not execution. Overnight developments—Asian and European market moves, futures positioning, news flow, and economic data releases—create the initial conditions for the session. During this window, the professional trader builds scenario awareness rather than directional conviction.

The key premarket questions:
- Where did futures settle relative to key gamma concentrations?
- What is the overnight vol regime—compression or expansion?
- Are there scheduled catalysts (earnings, Fed speakers, data releases)?
- How does current positioning compare to recent historical patterns?

**Phase 2: The Opening Window (9:30–10:00 AM ET)**

Alma emphasizes that the first 20–30 minutes of the session are characterized by high intraday volatility as participants establish their 0DTE positions. This is not the time for impulsive entry—it is the time for observation. As Alma notes: "You better watch during the first 30–40 min, and wait for the market to decide where it wants to go, and to build a structure."[^bible-june16-advice]

The opening window reveals:
- Initial liquidity depth and spread conditions
- Whether overnight gaps are being accepted or rejected
- Early positioning flows and their directional bias
- The formation of retested support or resistance zones

**Phase 3: Midday Structure Building (10:00 AM–2:00 PM ET)**

The midday phase is where the day's true structure emerges. If the opening established a directional bias, midday often provides the retest or consolidation that validates (or invalidates) that bias. This is typically the lowest-volatility period of the session, which means:
- Mean-reversion strategies face compressed edge
- Breakout attempts may lack follow-through
- Positioning adjustments by institutional players create subtle flow patterns
- The "net drift" of the session becomes visible

**Phase 4: The Post-2:00 PM Window (2:00–3:30 PM ET)**

Alma identifies the period after 2:00 PM as the second primary intraday edge opportunity. As the session enters its final hours, several mechanical forces intensify:
- Charm decay accelerates, forcing hedge adjustments
- Institutional rebalancing flows concentrate
- Options expiry dynamics become more pronounced on expiration days
- Momentum strategies that have been suppressed during midday may reactivate

**Phase 5: The Close (3:30–4:00 PM ET)**

The final 30 minutes represent a distinct regime where closing auction dynamics, MOC (Market on Close) imbalances, and overnight risk positioning dominate. The close is not for initiating new directional trades—it is for risk management, position sizing for overnight holds, and capturing any final mechanical flows.

### The Timing Discipline

Alma's core timing insight is that "during the day there is only one intraday edge opportunity, two at max. Usually during the AM, and after 2 PM."[^bible-june16-advice] This constraint is not arbitrary—it reflects the reality that high-probability setups are rare, and the trader who attempts to extract edge from every price wiggle becomes the source of edge for more patient participants.

The disciplined trader treats most of the trading day as a waiting game. Observation and preparation dominate action. When a setup does align with pre-defined criteria, execution becomes mechanical rather than discretionary.

## 7.2 Premarket Planning: The Scenario Template

Before the market opens, the professional trader completes a structured planning template. This document—whether mental or written—creates the decision architecture that prevents emotional improvisation during the session.

### The Alma Premarket Framework

Based on Alma's June 16, 2025 guidance, the premarket plan must answer these specific questions:[^bible-june16-advice]

**1. Daily Budget Definition**

"Decide your daily budget, including your loss limit, and also your profit limit."[^bible-june16-advice] This is not a suggestion—it is a non-negotiable risk architecture. The daily budget includes:
- Maximum acceptable loss for the session (hard stop)
- Profit target that triggers position reduction or full exit
- Number of trades permitted (to prevent overtrading)
- Maximum position size as a percentage of capital

**2. Side Selection and Familiarity**

"Decide which side (long or short) you 'feel,' which side you are familiar with to read."[^bible-june16-advice] This is an admission that not all market conditions suit every trader's strengths. The professional knows:
- Which directional regimes they read most accurately
- Which setups historically produce their best results
- When to stand aside because conditions fall outside their competence zone

**3. Entry Conditions and Size**

"Based on the intraday setup, decide at what point and what conditions you will take your trade, and in what size."[^bible-june16-advice] Specificity is mandatory:
- Exact price levels or technical conditions for entry
- Confirmation signals required before execution
- Position sizing scaled to setup quality and conviction
- Scaling plan (full size at once or incremental entry)

**4. Invalidation Criteria**

"With this conscious planning you also determine what conditions would disprove your trade—and you take the loss."[^bible-june16-advice] The invalidation plan prevents the catastrophic behavior of defending a losing thesis:
- Price levels that invalidate the setup
- Time-based exits (if the setup hasn't triggered by X time, abandon it)
- Volatility regime shifts that change the edge calculation
- Maximum adverse excursion before mechanical exit

### Scenario Planning: Three-Path Framework

Every premarket plan should map three scenarios:

**Scenario A: Bullish Resolution**
- Trigger conditions: specific price levels, vol behavior, flow alignment
- Target objectives: measured move targets based on structure
- Position sizing: full, half, or quarter size based on setup quality
- Invalidation: price levels or conditions that void the bullish thesis

**Scenario B: Bearish Resolution**
- Trigger conditions: breakdown levels, vol expansion, flow divergence
- Target objectives: support zones or measured move projections
- Position sizing: scaled to volatility regime and conviction
- Invalidation: reclaim of key levels or vol compression

**Scenario C: Stand Aside**
- Conditions: unclear structure, conflicting signals, low-conviction environment
- Action: no trade, or paper trade for observation only
- Reassessment triggers: what would change to make this tradeable?

### The "Stand Aside" Conditions

Not every day offers tradeable edge. Alma's framework explicitly includes the option—and often the wisdom—of not trading. "If it doesn't give it to you, don't FOMO into the other side, but wait for the next one or leave the desk, and leave it for tomorrow. You are not obligated to give your money to the market makers."[^bible-june16-advice]

Stand-aside conditions include:
- Opening volatility without clear structural resolution
- News-driven price action with unpredictable follow-through
- Positioning extremes that suggest mean reversion but without timing clarity
- Personal factors (fatigue, distraction, emotional state)
- Market structure that falls outside your competence zone

## 7.3 Position Sizing, Trade Frequency, and Overtrading Avoidance

The premarket plan defines the daily budget, but execution discipline determines whether that budget survives the session. Alma's framework treats position sizing and trade frequency as interconnected risk controls, not independent decisions. The trader who masters sizing but overtrades will eventually bleed to death; the trader who limits frequency but sizes recklessly will suffer catastrophic drawdowns.

### The Daily Budget as a Hard Constraint

Alma's June 16 guidance is explicit: "Decide your daily budget, including your loss limit, and also your profit limit."[^bible-june16-advice] This dual limit—loss *and* profit—distinguishes professional risk management from amateur hope. The profit limit prevents the greed spiral that transforms winning sessions into break-even or losing ones. The loss limit prevents the revenge-trading cascade that destroys accounts.

The daily budget framework operates on several interconnected dimensions:

**Capital-at-Risk Per Trade**: Professional traders typically risk 0.5% to 2% of account equity on any single trade. In high-volatility or low-conviction regimes, this should compress toward the lower bound. The key insight is that position size scales with setup quality and regime clarity—not with desire for profit or need to recover losses.

**Trade Frequency Caps**: Alma's observation that "during the day there is only one intraday edge opportunity, two at max"[^bible-june16-advice] imposes a natural frequency limit. The trader who attempts three, four, or five trades per day is either operating in exceptional market conditions or manufacturing edge where none exists. Most professional intraday traders target one to two high-quality setups per session.

**Session Shutdown Triggers**: The daily budget must include automatic shutdown conditions:
- Hard loss limit reached: trading stops immediately, no exceptions
- Profit target reached: option to reduce size, move stops to breakeven, or stop trading
- Consecutive losses (typically 2-3): mandatory cooling-off period
- Emotional state deterioration: immediate desk departure

### The Overtrading Trap

Overtrading is not a character flaw—it is a structural risk of the intraday environment. The market is open six and a half hours. The trader's edge exists for perhaps twenty minutes of that time. The remaining six hours and ten minutes are filled with price movement that *looks* like opportunity but is actually noise designed to extract capital from impatient participants.

Alma's antidote is explicit: "The next thing you need to do is to wait, wait and wait, until the market gives it to you."[^bible-june16-advice] This waiting is not passive—it is active scanning for the specific conditions that validate the premarket plan. The disciplined trader has defined what "it" looks like; everything else is distraction.

Common overtrading patterns to recognize:

**The Opening Impulse**: The first thirty minutes produce dramatic price movement. The untrained trader sees this as "action" and feels compelled to participate. The trained trader recognizes this as position establishment by algos and institutions—movement designed to trap retail flow, not invite it.

**The Midday Boredom Trade**: After the opening volatility subsides, price often enters a compressed range. The bored trader manufactures reasons to trade—"it's building energy," "this level looks important," "I need to make something happen." The professional recognizes midday compression for what it is: a low-edge environment where the correct position size is zero.

**The Late-Day Chase**: As the session approaches 2:00 PM, missed opportunities from the morning create psychological pressure. The trader who sat out a valid morning setup may force an afternoon entry to "make up for it." This revenge-trading mindset guarantees poor execution at exactly the time when discipline matters most.

### Position Sizing Mechanics

Sizing decisions flow from the daily budget and setup quality:

**Full Size (100% of allocated risk)**: Reserved for setups with:
- Clear structural alignment (all three pillars confirming)
- Favorable risk/reward (typically 1:2.5 or better)
- Regime conditions matching the trader's competence zone
- No conflicting macro or event risk

**Half Size (50% of allocated risk)**: Used when:
- Setup quality is good but not exceptional
- Volatility regime is elevated, expanding risk
- Partial confirmation exists (two of three pillars)
- Time-of-day considerations (late-session entries carry overnight risk)

**Quarter Size (25% of allocated risk) or No Trade**: Appropriate when:
- Setup quality is marginal
- Conflicting signals exist in other timeframes
- Personal factors (fatigue, distraction) impair judgment
- Market structure is unclear or transitional

The sizing decision is made *before* entry, not after. Adjusting size based on how the trade "feels" after entry is a form of emotional trading that undermines the entire risk architecture.

### The Waiting Discipline

Alma's final instruction on execution timing: "If it doesn't give it to you, don't FOMO into the other side, but wait for the next one or leave the desk, and leave it for tomorrow."[^bible-june16-advice]

This discipline has several practical implementations:

**The One-Trade Rule**: For traders struggling with overtrading, a useful constraint is the one-trade rule: only one trade per day, period. This forces extreme selectivity and eliminates the "I'll just try one more" cascade.

**The Two-Hour Rule**: If no valid setup has triggered within the first two hours of the session, the probability of a high-quality afternoon setup decreases significantly. Consider standing aside for the remainder of the day.

**The Paper-Trade Alternative**: When conditions are unclear but potentially developing, paper-trade the setup rather than risking capital. This maintains engagement without risking capital in low-edge environments.

**Key Insight:** Position sizing and trade frequency are the twin pillars of account preservation. Master these before seeking alpha.

**Trading Implication:** Define your daily budget in advance, cap your trade frequency at two per day maximum, and treat every trade outside your premarket plan as a mistake—even if it profits.

## 7.4 Reading Daily Positioning

Daily bias starts with inventory, not opinion. Positioning context tells you where forced behavior is likely if spot or vol moves beyond tolerance zones.

Alma's process reads this as a probability surface: what is most likely to be hedged, what is most likely to be absorbed, and where fragility appears if regime shifts. This prevents overreaction to single prints and anchors the day in structure. The purpose is not omniscience. The purpose is to remove avoidable surprises.

The June 12, 2025 post-CPI analysis demonstrates this approach. Alma noted that "momentum and sentiment-wise, as long as SPX can close above 5955, ES=F above 5968.10, NDX above 21,549.18 and NQ=F above 21,603.46, pullback is not confirmed."[^bible-june12-cpi] These were not arbitrary levels—they were thresholds derived from positioning analysis that indicated where mechanical behavior would shift.

The post also highlighted a critical regime shift: "The SPX/yields beta flipped to positive just right ahead of CPI release... This marks a shift to a regime where yield moves are dominated by term-premium normalization."[^bible-june12-cpi] This structural reading provided context that headline-focused traders missed.

Positioning data reveals the convexity landscape before price moves through it. Where are the gamma concentrations? Where is the vanna exposure? How does charm decay through the session? These questions create a map of mechanical pressure points that operate regardless of news flow.

The trader who starts with positioning has visibility into forced flows. The trader who starts with headlines chases price after the move has already begun.

**Key Insight:** Positioning transforms intraday forecasting from narrative guessing to conditional mapping.

**Trading Implication:** Write the day as scenarios before the open, then update only when state variables materially change. Treat scenarios as probability distributions, not forecasts—size positions for uncertainty and respect your risk budget regardless of conviction.

## 7.5 Coding Pivots

A pivot is not just a horizontal line, it is a decision boundary with flow consequences. In Alma's framework, pivots are coded from structure: exposure concentration, flip proximity, and conditional volatility response.

The value of coding is consistency; it reduces emotional level-drawing and forces pre-trade logic. When price tests a pivot, the question is not "is this support?" but "did state confirm expected behavior?" If yes, execute with predefined risk. If not, downgrade the level and move on.

The March 26, 2025 analysis illustrates this with gamma considerations. Into Friday expiry, "the skew became longer, and the positioning created specific decision boundaries."[^bible-march26-gamma] These were not arbitrary lines but structural pivots where dealer hedging behavior would change character. The trader who coded these levels in advance could execute with confidence when price approached them.

Pivots fail. This is expected. The difference between amateur and professional is not perfect pivot prediction—it is what happens when the pivot breaks. The professional has predefined invalidation and rotates immediately. The amateur defends the level and compounds losses.

**Key Insight:** Pivots are executable hypotheses tied to flow behavior, not static chart decorations.

**Trading Implication:** Use fail-fast rules at pivots; invalidated structure should trigger immediate plan rotation, not thesis defense.

## 7.6 Momentum Confirmation: The Three-Pillar Checklist

Breakouts need participation from volatility and flow, not just price extension. Alma repeatedly flags this in intraday notes: without vol-state confirmation, many directional pushes are theater.

True confirmation usually includes follow-through in realized behavior, not immediate mean-revert snapback, plus alignment between spot and hedge pressure. In short-gamma or stress windows, this can happen quickly. In suppressive regimes, the same price move often dies on contact with liquidity. Confirmation is therefore a state check, not an emotional reaction.

### The Momentum Confirmation Checklist

**Pillar 1: Spot Behavior**
- Price extension beyond a defined pivot or range boundary
- Sustained move beyond the initial breakout level (not immediate rejection)
- Volume participation supporting the directional push
- Acceptance of the new price level (consolidation above/below, not immediate return)

**Pillar 2: Volatility Confirmation**
- Realized volatility expanding in the direction of the move
- Implied volatility responding appropriately (not compressing during a breakout)
- Term structure behavior consistent with the directional thesis
- Skew shifts that validate the move (not fighting it)

**Pillar 3: Flow Alignment**
- Dealer hedging flows supporting the directional push
- Options flow consistent with the price movement
- Institutional positioning adjusting in the direction of the move
- Absence of contra-flow that would absorb the momentum

The August 29, 2025 analysis provides a counter-example where momentum confirmation failed despite price extension. "Vomma supply is on (grey line), and very heavy... Through vomma supply, concentrated on the right-tail of the distribution, they create a 'braking' effect, in order to extend this melt-up momentum."[^bible-aug29-vomma]

In this environment, "long vol/long delta strategies can't really capitalize on this move, while locking the cheapness of the downside hedges."[^bible-aug29-vomma] The price continued higher, but without genuine vol confirmation, the move was mechanically fragile. The secondary effect was "cooling down quant algos that take the velocity of the momentum into account, so they measure lower risk."[^bible-aug29-vomma]

Momentum traders often confuse price extension with momentum. True momentum has vol expansion, flow alignment, and persistence. False momentum has price moving while vol compresses and flow fights it. The distinction determines whether to add size or take profits.

**Key Insight:** Price extension without vol/flow confirmation is often a false breakout.

**Trading Implication:** Require multi-signal confirmation before adding size to momentum trades.

## 7.7 The Reversion Trap Checklist

Reversion edges disappear when structure shifts faster than your playbook. A dip can look exhausted while short-gamma chase flow is only beginning. A squeeze can look overextended while vol compression and charm keep adding support.

Alma's trap language is useful because it centers mismatch: your strategy assumptions versus current mechanics. Most losses in this category come from using yesterday's regime template on today's tape. Reversion is a powerful tool, but only inside the right state.

### The Reversion Trap Warning Signs

**Warning 1: Volatility Regime Mismatch**
- Attempting mean reversion in a trending, vol-expanding environment
- Fading breakouts during periods of heavy positioning imbalance
- Ignoring vomma or vanna effects that extend moves beyond "normal" ranges

**Warning 2: Timing Without Structure**
- Entering mean reversion trades without defined invalidation levels
- Fading moves based on "overbought" indicators alone
- Ignoring the mechanical forces (gamma, charm, vanna) that sustain trends

**Warning 3: False Mean Reversion Signals**
- Price consolidation mistaken for exhaustion
- Temporary pullbacks within larger trending structures
- "Rubber band" indicators stretched but not broken

The June 12, 2025 post demonstrated proper reversion discipline. Alma noted that the "reversion model gave the 80%+ signal for a reversion what I've been waiting for to go short,"[^bible-june12-cpi] but immediately qualified this with threshold conditions that had to hold. The model provided the directional bias, but the execution required confirmation.

The reversion model signaled 80%+ probability on June 12, providing specific targets. But Alma consistently caveated these signals with confirmation requirements: momentum and sentiment thresholds that had to hold, or the pullback was not confirmed. This is the anti-trap discipline: having a directional bias while requiring evidence before deployment.

**Key Insight:** Mean reversion fails hardest when used against active convexity pressure.

**Trading Implication:** Before fading a move, verify that regime variables support reversion; if not, preserve capital and wait.

## 7.8 Common Failure Modes and Behavioral Guardrails

Every intraday trader develops a personal catalog of mistakes. The professional catalogs them systematically; the amateur repeats them indefinitely. Alma's framework includes explicit recognition of failure patterns and the behavioral guardrails that prevent their recurrence.

### Failure Mode 1: Opening Impulse Entry

**The Pattern**: The market gaps higher or lower at the open. FOMO kicks in. The trader enters immediately, afraid of "missing the move," only to watch the initial direction reverse as the true session structure emerges.

**Why It Happens**: The opening print feels like information, but it is often noise. The first 20–30 minutes represent position establishment by algos and institutions, not directional commitment. The retail trader sees the move; the professional sees the positioning.

**The Alma Antidote**: "You better watch during the first 30–40 min, and wait for the market to decide where it wants to go, and to build a structure."[^bible-june16-advice] This is not optional advice—it is a survival requirement.

**Behavioral Guardrails**:
- No entries in the first 30 minutes except for pre-planned overnight setups
- Use the opening window exclusively for observation and level confirmation
- If an opening move triggers FOMO, automatically label it as "untradeable" and step back
- Require a retest of the opening range before considering any directional position

### Failure Mode 2: Midday Chop Participation

**The Pattern**: After the morning volatility, price enters a tight range. The bored trader manufactures reasons to trade—drawing trendlines on noise, identifying "patterns" in randomness, forcing entries to "stay engaged."

**Why It Happens**: Trading is psychologically rewarding. The brain craves the dopamine hit of action. Midday chop exploits this by providing just enough movement to suggest opportunity while delivering only transaction costs and frustration.

**The Alma Antidote**: The recognition that "during the day there is only one intraday edge opportunity, two at max"[^bible-june16-advice] implies that most of the day is *not* for trading. The professional accepts boredom as the price of edge.

**Behavioral Guardrails**:
- Declare midday (10:30 AM–2:00 PM) a "no new position" zone unless exceptional conditions develop
- If tempted to trade midday, check: would I take this trade with ten times the size? If not, skip it
- Use midday for administrative tasks, journal review, or physical movement away from screens
- Set a physical timer; when it rings, reassess whether any actual edge has emerged

### Failure Mode 3: Late-Day Chasing

**The Pattern**: The trader missed the morning setup. As the session approaches 2:00 PM, psychological pressure builds to "make something happen." A marginal setup develops. The trader takes it, often at poor prices, and watches it fail.

**Why It Happens**: Loss aversion operates in reverse: the fear of missing out on daily profit targets drives poor decisions. The trader who planned for one quality trade feels compelled to take two when the first doesn't materialize.

**The Alma Antidote**: "If it doesn't give it to you, don't FOMO into the other side, but wait for the next one or leave the desk, and leave it for tomorrow."[^bible-june16-advice]

**Behavioral Guardrails**:
- After 2:00 PM, only take setups that meet *stricter* criteria than morning trades
- Require explicit written justification for any post-2:00 PM entry
- If the morning produced no valid trades, reduce afternoon size by 50% or stand aside entirely
- Set a hard stop time (e.g., 3:00 PM) after which no new positions are initiated

### Failure Mode 4: Violating the Daily Budget

**The Pattern**: The trader hits the daily loss limit but continues trading, convinced the next trade will "make it back." Or the trader hits the profit target but keeps pushing for more, giving back gains.

**Why It Happens**: The daily budget feels arbitrary in the moment. The trader reasons: "I can afford one more loss" or "I'm hot, why stop now?" This reasoning is always wrong.

**The Alma Antidote**: "Decide your daily budget, including your loss limit, and also your profit limit."[^bible-june16-advice] Both limits are non-negotiable.

**Behavioral Guardrails**:
- Use platform settings to enforce hard stops when daily limits are reached
- Physically leave the trading space when the daily budget is exhausted (win or lose)
- Review the session the next day before deciding whether limits were appropriately set
- Track "budget violations" as a separate metric; three violations in a week triggers a trading halt

### Failure Mode 5: Trading Outside Your Competence Zone

**The Pattern**: The market enters a regime the trader doesn't understand—perhaps heavy vomma suppression, or a vanna-dominated environment. The trader trades anyway, applying familiar techniques to unfamiliar mechanics.

**Why It Happens**: Overconfidence from past success. The trader assumes competence in one regime transfers to all regimes.

**The Alma Antidote**: "Decide which side (long or short) you 'feel,' which side you are familiar with to read."[^bible-june16-advice] Implicit in this is the corollary: don't trade what you can't read.

**Behavioral Guardrails**:
- Maintain a personal "regime journal" tracking which environments produce your best and worst results
- When unfamiliar mechanics dominate (vomma suppression, extreme gamma concentration, etc.), reduce size or stand aside
- Study unfamiliar regimes on paper before risking capital
- Accept that missing a move in an unreadable regime is not a mistake—it is discipline

**Key Insight:** Failure modes are predictable; the professional anticipates them with guardrails, not willpower.

**Trading Implication:** Write your failure modes and guardrails on a card placed beside your monitor. When a guardrail triggers, obey it mechanically—no deliberation, no exceptions.

## 7.10 Do Not Marry a Side

Attachment is the hidden tax in intraday trading. Alma's core discipline is conditional thinking: if this, then that, and if invalidated, rotate without ego. The market does not reward consistency of opinion; it rewards consistency of process.

A trader who can abandon a thesis early often keeps enough capital to exploit the real move later. This is not indecision, it is risk intelligence. Probabilistic flexibility is the final edge that keeps the whole framework alive.

The June 16, 2025 trading advice post crystallizes this discipline. After defining the daily budget, side selection, entry conditions, and invalidation criteria, Alma emphasizes the final step: "The next thing you need to do is to wait, wait and wait, until the market gives it to you."[^bible-june16-advice]

This waiting is not passive—it is active patience, scanning for the specific conditions that validate the premarket plan. If those conditions never materialize, the disciplined trader accepts the absence of edge and moves to the next opportunity.

The emotional trader improvises under pressure. The mechanical trader executes a plan made in calm.

**Key Insight:** Process loyalty beats directional loyalty.

**Trading Implication:** Predefine invalidation, honor it quickly, and redeploy only when a new state is confirmed.

## 7.11 End-of-Chapter Checklist: Risk Budgeting and Non-Determinism

Before every trading session, verify:

**Risk Budget Verification:**
- [ ] Daily loss limit defined and documented
- [ ] Profit target defined (not just "as much as possible")
- [ ] Maximum position size calculated as percentage of capital
- [ ] Maximum number of trades for the session established
- [ ] Overnight risk budget defined (if holding positions)

**Premarket Planning Verification:**
- [ ] Three scenarios mapped (bullish, bearish, stand aside)
- [ ] Specific entry conditions defined with price levels
- [ ] Invalidation criteria explicit for each scenario
- [ ] Position sizing scaled to setup quality

## Chapter Close

The intraday playbook is not a crystal ball. It is a decision framework for trading the state in front of you, not the state you wish existed.

Reading positioning before the open creates scenario awareness. Coding pivots provides executable structure. Requiring momentum confirmation filters false breakouts. Recognizing reversion traps prevents fighting convexity. And refusing to marry a side preserves capital for when the real move arrives.

Alma's framework treats trading as engineering, not prophecy. The goal is not to predict every tick but to align with mechanical flows when they are clear and stand aside when they are not. This is probabilistic discipline: edge over time, not certainty on every trade.

The traders who survive and prosper are not the ones with perfect forecasts. They are the ones with robust processes that adapt when conditions change. The intraday playbook is that process—conditional, mechanical, and relentlessly focused on the state that actually exists.
